### POST 5 — THE SCALE THREAD (1/10)

10 supply chain facts that sound fake but are 100% true. 🧵

---

**1/10**

90% of global trade moves by sea.

If shipping stopped for 2 weeks, supermarket shelves would be empty in most developed countries.

Not in years. In weeks.

---

**2/10**

The "Bullwhip Effect" means a 5% increase in consumer demand can become a 40% order surge at the manufacturer level.

P&G discovered this with Pampers in the 1990s. Their factory was in chaos while consumer demand was perfectly stable.

---

**3/10**

The Ever Given — the ship that blocked the Suez Canal for 6 days in 2021 — was carrying $9.6 BILLION worth of cargo.

One ship. One sandbank. Global shortages.

---

**4/10**

Apple keeps a maximum of 5 days of inventory on hand.

Most companies keep 30–90 days.

One supplier failure = global iPhone shortage.

That's their calculated bet. Every quarter.

---

**5/10**

During COVID, the US had a shortage of chicken wings.

Not chickens. Wings specifically.

Restaurants closed → demand shifted to wings for takeout → processors couldn't rebalance cut lines in time.

Supply chains aren't logical. They're political.

---

**6/10**

The Panama Canal handles 5% of global maritime trade.

In 2023, drought reduced water levels so severely that 140 ships were waiting at anchor at any given moment.

Drought. Not a war. Not a cyberattack. Just less rain.

---

**7/10**

The "last mile" — getting a package from a local warehouse to your front door — is the most expensive segment of the entire supply chain per km.

It costs more to move a parcel 10 km to your door than to move it 10,000 km across an ocean.

---

**8/10**

The US has a Strategic Petroleum Reserve — a buffer for energy supply shocks.

There is no equivalent reserve for semiconductors, rare earth metals, or critical pharmaceuticals.

This is why your PS5 was impossible to find in 2021.

---

**9/10**

"Safety stock" is the inventory you hold just in case demand or supply varies.

Most companies calculate it using average demand.

They should use demand variability.

The difference between those two numbers is the difference between a real buffer and a pile of expensive waste.

### POST 11 — LOGIC REVEAL: THE BULLWHIP EFFECT

Just finished coding the Bullwhip Effect algorithm.

Here's what it does in the game:

You over-order by 10% at the retail level →
Wholesaler panics, adds a 15% buffer →
Distributor sees the spike, adds 20% →
Manufacturer ramps capacity →

Then demand normalises.

You now have 3× the inventory you need. Cash is gone. Storage is maxed. A demand dip hits.

This is how real companies collapse during "normal" conditions.

I broke the inventory logic last night.

### POST 36 — SURVIVAL GUIDE THREAD (1/12)

The complete guide to surviving a Supply Chain Crisis. 🧵

---

**1/12**

The first thing you lose in a crisis is visibility.

You don't know how bad it is. You don't know how long it will last.

The temptation is to react aggressively — over-order, air freight everything, switch suppliers immediately.

This is how the Bullwhip Effect starts.

Rule 1: Do not place emergency orders before you understand the scope of the disruption.

---

**2/12**

Understand your exposure tiers.

**Tier 1:** Your direct suppliers. You probably know these.
**Tier 2:** Your suppliers' suppliers. You probably don't.
**Tier 3:** Raw material sources. Almost nobody actively tracks these.

Most supply chain crises originate at Tier 2 or Tier 3. The shock travels downstream to you before you can see it coming.

---

**3/12**

Cash is your only universal buffer.

Inventory buffers specific SKUs. Cash buffers everything.

Companies with strong cash positions during COVID rerouted, air-freighted, and dual-sourced their way through. Companies that were over-leveraged couldn't move.

Cash flow management IS supply chain risk management.

---

**4/12**

Know your P90 lead time — not your average lead time.

P90 = the time within which 90% of your shipments arrive.

Most procurement teams use average lead time for safety stock calculations.

This is why they always run out. Average isn't protection. Distribution is.

---

**5/12**

Have a pre-qualified backup supplier for every critical category.

Not a "we know someone." A signed agreement with agreed pricing, minimum order quantity, and lead time — ready to activate.

When your primary fails, you need to move in hours. Not weeks.

---

**6/12**

Don't confuse stockout risk with overstock risk.

Most people obsess over stockouts. Overstock kills more companies quietly.

Dead inventory = frozen cash + storage cost + eventual write-down.

It's a slow bleed that compounds invisibly until it can't be ignored.

---

**7/12**

The safest supply chain is not the one with the most inventory.

It's the one with the fastest response time.

Visibility + flexibility > bulk + buffer.

Every time.

---

**8/12**

Air freight is not a supply chain strategy. It's a planning failure made expensive.

Every emergency air shipment is evidence that something upstream went wrong: a forecast, a safety stock calculation, a supplier assessment.

Use it when you must. But always trace back to what made it necessary.

---

**9/12**

The Bullwhip Effect can be dampened by sharing real sell-through data with your suppliers.

Not your orders. Actual consumer demand.

When your supplier can see what's actually moving off shelves, they can plan. When they can only see your orders, they react.

Information sharing is the highest-ROI supply chain investment available.

---

**10/12**

Resilience is not redundancy.

Redundancy = having two of everything. Expensive. Wasteful. Often impossible.

Resilience = being able to switch quickly when one option fails. Different problem, different solution.

Build decision speed into your process. The ability to pivot in 24 hours is worth more than a secondary warehouse.

---

**11/12**

Every supply chain crisis eventually becomes a liquidity crisis.

Whether it starts as a supplier failure, a port closure, or a demand collapse — it ends as a cash problem.

Treat your cash position as your most critical supply chain KPI. In a crisis, it is the only KPI that matters.

---

**12/12**

The best time to stress-test your supply chain is when nothing is wrong.

It will show you exactly where you'd break — before reality does it for you.
